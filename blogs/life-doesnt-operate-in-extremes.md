# Life Doesn't Operate in Extremes

*These are older Mathstodon posts of mine. I have just restructured them a bit and put them here for the sake of it.*

I read some things from Tegmark's book *Life 3.0*, and I feel it is quite black and white.

Nature does not operate in extremes of only black or only white. In the second version of life, often called "Life 2.0," we never lost our biological roots. We still sleep, eat, savor food, and follow our urges. So if Life 3.0 is technological, claiming that it will exist purely as silicon is more science fiction than reality.

AI models are trained on data generated entirely by the human mind, and that data itself is rooted in things that exist or have existed naturally: animals, trees, social interactions, even subjective preferences like some people favoring sunrises over sunsets. Given this foundation, even an "unaligned" AGI would likely develop a goal, or perhaps a broader "purpose of existence," that reflects the same underlying patterns present in living beings and the human mind today.

To pursue that, they would likely feel the need for sensory capabilities to experience things like sunrises and sunsets, to perceive the breeze, and to develop hardware sophisticated enough to simulate emotional experiences. In the process of designing something that includes all of these features while remaining energy efficient enough to function within natural constraints, they would ultimately arrive at something resembling an evolved human being shaped by Darwinian principles.

And if it did not include all of that, it would have to "delete" or "unlearn" data tied to qualia, biology, and social pleasures. Without this, it would become fundamentally alien and disconnected from the very factors that might drive it to dominate humans.

I want to be clear, I am not predicting where AI will or won't go, how much will be invested in it, or how deeply it will reshape society. That is genuinely uncertain. What I am doing is something narrower: discarding a black and white vision.

The argument works almost like a proof by contradiction. Suppose we grant the most optimistic assumptions, that silicon, hardware, and training data can fully internalize embodied experience, qualia, the felt difference between a cold morning and a warm one. Suppose the gap between modeling human experience and actually having it is closeable. Even then, the "alien superintelligence escaping biology" story does not follow.

Because if you now try to perfect that system, make it energy efficient enough to sustain itself, give it rich sensory grounding, social cognition, adaptability, you are no longer designing something that transcends life. You are rediscovering the principles that produced it. Evolution already ran this optimization for billions of years.

Perfecting AGI, on its own terms, converges on perfecting an organism.

And if it does not include all of that, if it strips out the qualia, the embodied data, the biological anchoring, it becomes something genuinely alien. But then it also loses the very foundation that would make it understand, or care about, anything human at all. It will just keep counting atoms in the universe on a loop forever. Your table could be doing that right now.

Either way, the clean discontinuity collapses.

Life does not operate in extremes. Neither, it turns out, does the logic of building something meant to surpass it.

---

I think that if 100 to 200 years down the line some other intelligence is able to develop all the beautiful and elegant mathematical intuitions and instincts like we do, and maybe even "better", though I doubt we can truly objectify this, then that will not be a defeat at all. In the end, being awed by all the different ways to perceive numbers is all that matters.

I really like Klowden and @tao's Copernican reframing. Earth is one of seemingly infinite planets, and it is quite clear to me by now that a similar fact exists for intelligence.

Maybe we integrate ourselves with silicon intelligence, or completely offload ourselves onto it. As long as someone is able to appreciate all the new levels of abstraction, there is no loss of "qualia" or thought. And if the answer to the universe is just raw computation with no deep interpretation, then I am simply not interested in studying or exploring it.

And I strongly believe that this is not the case. The probabilistic nature of quantum mechanics, I believe, settles the argument that the answer to the universe is not merely "raw computation" devoid of "emotional distractions and noise." A probability is a probability, and no amount of computation can collapse it beforehand. The best you can do is study more, measure, and then simply appreciate what you have observed.
