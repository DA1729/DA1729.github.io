# Offloading and the Compression of Knowledge

*These are older Mathstodon posts of mine. I have just restructured them a bit and put them here for the sake of it.*

I encountered a really interesting hypothesis a while back. Over the course of evolution, our digestive system initially increased in size but later shrank. It is theorized that humans began offloading some of the digestive workload to external processes, such as using fire to cook food and break down starches.

Similarly, while human brains increased in size over much of our evolutionary history, there is evidence they may now be shrinking. My theory is that we have begun outsourcing some cognitive tasks to external systems, such as silicon based technologies and AI.

This sits well with my earlier ideas.

Following that analogy a little further: proof writing might become more of a hobby, or perhaps a recreational ritual, something like gardening, garden farming, and hunting.

I have this whole theory of humans always finding new ways to condense and compress the knowledge they gather. Like how we invented writing and books, which was followed by computers and high density storage devices.

Now that we have advanced just enough, all the proof writing and mathematical rigour across all sorts of fields, especially mathematical physics, was posing more and more friction against free and intuitive thinking, essentially stopping us from developing elegant instincts. It was also making all of these fields less and less accessible. By less accessible, I mean one would have to study a high volume of text and "knowledge" before developing just enough instinct to contribute, to see something new, to take unconventional approaches.

I personally feel that mathematics has become much more accessible for me than it was two years back, when these LLMs did not perform as well as they do today. And I have thought enough about it to not give all the credit to my own increased maturity over the past two years. Even accounting for that maturity, I can totally imagine the amount of inaccessibility I would still have been facing.

I shall write more about this compression of gathered knowledge theory of mine.

Around the same time, Terence Tao wrote something that fits this almost too well:

> Just as modern societies no longer consider raw food ingredients as constituting a meal, I predict that mathematical research culture will cease considering "raw", "undigested" proofs as constituting a solution to a problem, and focus more on how the field as a whole, as opposed to just the problem itself, is enriched by the contribution.
