Ring-LWE and CKKS: Mathematical Foundations for Homomorphic Encryption

From LWE to Ring-LWE: Why Rings?

So we all know that standard LWE works with vectors in Zqn\mathbb{Z}_q^nZqn​. An LWE sample looks like (a,b)(\mathbf{a}, b)(a,b) where b=⟨a,s⟩+e+Δmb = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{s} \rangle + e + \Delta mb=⟨a,s⟩+e+Δm. This is great and all, but there are some serious practical issues:

  1. Key Size: The secret key s\mathbf{s}s has nnn elements
  2. Ciphertext Size: Each ciphertext is (n+1)(n+1)(n+1) elements
  3. Operations: Matrix-vector multiplications are expensive

Ring-LWE fixes this by moving from vectors to polynomials. Instead of working in Zqn\mathbb{Z}_q^nZqn​, we work in the polynomial ring Rq=Zq[X]/(f(X))R_q = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(f(X))Rq​=Zq​[X]/(f(X)) for some polynomial f(X)f(X)f(X).

Now here’s the key insight: a polynomial of degree n−1n-1n−1 can be represented by nnn coefficients, just like a vector of length nnn. But polynomial arithmetic gives us way more structure to work with.

A Ring-LWE sample looks like:

(a(X),b(X)) where b(X)=a(X)⋅s(X)+e(X)+Δ⋅m(X) mod f(X)(a(X), b(X)) \text{ where } b(X) = a(X) \cdot s(X) + e(X) + \Delta \cdot m(X) \bmod f(X)(a(X),b(X)) where b(X)=a(X)⋅s(X)+e(X)+Δ⋅m(X)modf(X)

The magic happens because polynomial multiplication in RqR_qRq​ can be done super efficiently using Number Theoretic Transform (NTT), which is basically FFT over finite fields.

Cyclotomic Polynomials and Algebraic Structure

Now, the choice of f(X)f(X)f(X) is crucial. We typically use f(X)=Xn+1f(X) = X^n + 1f(X)=Xn+1 where nnn is a power of 2. This isn’t random - Xn+1X^n + 1Xn+1 is the 2n2n2n-th cyclotomic polynomial Φ2n(X)\Phi_{2n}(X)Φ2n​(X).

What are Cyclotomic Polynomials?

Think of cyclotomic polynomials as the “primes” of polynomial rings. Just like how prime numbers are the indivisible building blocks of integers, cyclotomic polynomials are irreducible polynomials that can’t be factored further over the rationals.

But here’s the cool part - while prime numbers feel abstract and random, cyclotomic polynomials have this beautiful geometric interpretation. The mmm-th cyclotomic polynomial Φm(X)\Phi_m(X)Φm​(X) “knows about” the mmm-sided regular polygon!

Visualizing the Geometric Picture

Imagine you’re standing at the center of a circle, looking at the vertices of a regular 2n2n2n-sided polygon inscribed in that circle. Each vertex corresponds to a 2n2n2n-th root of unity - a complex number ζ2nk=e2πik/2n\zeta_{2n}^k = e^{2\pi i k / 2n}ζ2nk​=e2πik/2n.

Now, most of these vertices are “derived” - if you know where vertex 1 is, you can get vertex 2 by just squaring it, vertex 3 by cubing it, etc. But some vertices are primitive - they can’t be obtained as powers of vertices from smaller polygons.

The cyclotomic polynomial Φ2n(X)\Phi_{2n}(X)Φ2n​(X) is exactly the polynomial whose roots are these primitive vertices!

For our specific case where m=2nm = 2nm=2n and nnn is a power of 2:

Φ2n(X)=Xn+1\Phi_{2n}(X) = X^n + 1Φ2n​(X)=Xn+1

The primitive 2n2n2n-th roots of unity are exactly the odd powers: {ζ2n,ζ2n3,ζ2n5,…,ζ2n2n−1}\{\zeta_{2n}, \zeta_{2n}^3, \zeta_{2n}^5, \ldots, \zeta_{2n}^{2n-1}\}{ζ2n​,ζ2n3​,ζ2n5​,…,ζ2n2n−1​}.

Why odd powers? Because if kkk is even, then ζ2nk=(ζn)k/2\zeta_{2n}^k = (\zeta_{n})^{k/2}ζ2nk​=(ζn​)k/2 is actually an nnn-th root of unity, so it “belongs” to a smaller polygon!

The Ring Structure: Why Xn=−1X^n = -1Xn=−1 is Magic

Working in R=Z[X]/(Xn+1)R = \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^n + 1)R=Z[X]/(Xn+1) means we’re doing polynomial arithmetic with the rule that Xn=−1X^n = -1Xn=−1. This seems weird at first, but it’s actually brilliant!

Think of it this way: in regular polynomial multiplication, if you multiply two polynomials of degree ddd, you get a polynomial of degree 2d2d2d. That’s annoying for storage - your polynomials keep getting bigger.

But with the rule Xn=−1X^n = -1Xn=−1, any polynomial automatically “wraps around” to stay within degree n−1n-1n−1. It’s like doing arithmetic on a clock, but instead of 12+1=112 + 1 = 112+1=1, we have Xn=−1X^n = -1Xn=−1.

Here’s the intuition for why it’s −1-1−1 and not 000: remember those primitive 2n2n2n-th roots of unity? They satisfy ζ2n=1\zeta^{2n} = 1ζ2n=1, which means (ζn)2=1(\zeta^n)^2 = 1(ζn)2=1. Since ζn≠1\zeta^n \neq 1ζn=1 (that would make it an nnn-th root), we must have ζn=−1\zeta^n = -1ζn=−1.

The beautiful structure we get:

  1. Wrapping Multiplication: Xi⋅Xj=X(i+j) mod nX^i \cdot X^j = X^{(i+j) \bmod n}Xi⋅Xj=X(i+j)modn if i+j<ni+j < ni+j<n, otherwise Xi⋅Xj=−X(i+j) mod nX^i \cdot X^j = -X^{(i+j) \bmod n}Xi⋅Xj=−X(i+j)modn
  2. FFT-Friendly: The Number Theoretic Transform works perfectly because our roots of unity are evenly spaced around the circle
  3. Lattice Structure: The ring elements correspond to lattice points with special geometric properties

The automorphism group is isomorphic to (Z/2nZ)∗(\mathbb{Z}/2n\mathbb{Z})^*(Z/2nZ)∗, which has order nnn. Each automorphism σk\sigma_kσk​ is defined by σk:X↦Xk\sigma_k: X \mapsto X^kσk​:X↦Xk where gcd⁡(k,2n)=1\gcd(k, 2n) = 1gcd(k,2n)=1.

The Chinese Remainder Theorem Perspective

This is where the magic really happens, and it’s the key insight that makes CKKS so powerful. Let me give you the intuition first, then the math.

The “Multiple Personalities” View

Imagine you have a polynomial p(X)p(X)p(X). Instead of thinking of it as a single mathematical object, the Chinese Remainder Theorem says you can think of it as having nnn different “personalities” - one for each root of Xn+1X^n + 1Xn+1.

It’s like how Clark Kent and Superman are the same person, just in different contexts. Your polynomial p(X)p(X)p(X) is “the same” as the vector (p(ζ1),p(ζ2),…,p(ζn))(p(\zeta_1), p(\zeta_2), \ldots, p(\zeta_n))(p(ζ1​),p(ζ2​),…,p(ζn​)) where the ζi\zeta_iζi​ are the roots of Xn+1X^n + 1Xn+1.

Mathematically, this gives us an isomorphism:

C[X]/(Xn+1)≅Cn\mathbb{C}[X]/(X^n + 1) \cong \mathbb{C}^nC[X]/(Xn+1)≅Cn

Any polynomial p(X)∈C[X]/(Xn+1)p(X) \in \mathbb{C}[X]/(X^n + 1)p(X)∈C[X]/(Xn+1) is uniquely determined by its evaluations:

p(X)↔(p(ζ2n),p(ζ2n3),…,p(ζ2n2n−1))p(X) \leftrightarrow (p(\zeta_{2n}), p(\zeta_{2n}^3), \ldots, p(\zeta_{2n}^{2n-1}))p(X)↔(p(ζ2n​),p(ζ2n3​),…,p(ζ2n2n−1​))

Why This is Revolutionary for Computation

Here’s the killer insight: polynomial operations become pointwise vector operations!

Want to add two polynomials? Just add their evaluation vectors component-wise. Want to multiply? Multiply component-wise. This is called SIMD (Single Instruction, Multiple Data) - you get nnn operations for the price of one.

Complex Conjugate Pairs: The CKKS Packing Trick

Here’s where CKKS gets really clever. Remember our 2n2n2n-sided polygon? The vertices come in conjugate pairs - if you have a vertex at angle θ\thetaθ, you also have one at angle −θ-\theta−θ.

This means if ζ\zetaζ is a root of Xn+1X^n + 1Xn+1, then so is ζ‾\overline{\zeta}ζ​ (its complex conjugate).

Now here’s the packing magic: if we restrict to polynomials with real coefficients (which is what we do in practice), then evaluating our polynomial at conjugate pairs gives conjugate values:

p(ζ)=p(ζ‾)‾p(\zeta) = \overline{p(\overline{\zeta})}p(ζ)=p(ζ​)​

This means we only need to store half the evaluation vector! If we know p(ζ)p(\zeta)p(ζ), we automatically know p(ζ‾)=p(ζ)‾p(\overline{\zeta}) = \overline{p(\zeta)}p(ζ​)=p(ζ)​.

Result: We can pack n/2n/2n/2 complex numbers into a single polynomial of degree n−1n-1n−1. It’s like getting a 2x compression for free, just by exploiting the symmetry of the roots!

CKKS Encoding: Complex Numbers in Polynomials

The CKKS scheme exploits this conjugate structure beautifully. Think of it as a universal translator between two languages: the language of polynomials and the language of complex vectors.

The Encoding Intuition

Imagine you have n/2n/2n/2 complex numbers that represent, say, the pixels of an image or the coefficients of a Fourier transform. You want to encrypt them homomorphically, but Ring-LWE only knows how to encrypt polynomials.

CKKS says: “No problem! I’ll convert your complex vector into a polynomial, encrypt that polynomial, and when you do operations on the polynomial, they’ll automatically happen to your original complex numbers.”

It’s like having a magical box where you put in a complex vector, it gets converted to a polynomial, encrypted, and when you add two such boxes, the encrypted polynomials add in a way that makes the underlying complex vectors add too.

Canonical Embedding: The Universal Translator

The canonical embedding σ\sigmaσ is our universal translator. It takes a polynomial p(X)=∑i=0n−1piXip(X) = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} p_i X^ip(X)=∑i=0n−1​pi​Xi and evaluates it at all our special roots:

σ(p)=(p(ζ2n),p(ζ2n3),…,p(ζ2n2n−1))\sigma(p) = (p(\zeta_{2n}), p(\zeta_{2n}^3), \ldots, p(\zeta_{2n}^{2n-1}))σ(p)=(p(ζ2n​),p(ζ2n3​),…,p(ζ2n2n−1​))

This is like asking: “If this polynomial were a person, what would it say when you ask it about each of these nnn roots?”

Encoding Complex Vectors

To encode a vector z=(z0,z1,…,zn/2−1)∈Cn/2\mathbf{z} = (z_0, z_1, \ldots, z_{n/2-1}) \in \mathbb{C}^{n/2}z=(z0​,z1​,…,zn/2−1​)∈Cn/2, we:

  1. Extend to Conjugates: Create z~=(z0,…,zn/2−1,zn/2−1‾,…,z0‾)∈Cn\tilde{\mathbf{z}} = (z_0, \ldots, z_{n/2-1}, \overline{z_{n/2-1}}, \ldots, \overline{z_0}) \in \mathbb{C}^nz~=(z0​,…,zn/2−1​,zn/2−1​​,…,z0​​)∈Cn
  2. Inverse Canonical Embedding: Compute p=σ−1(z~)p = \sigma^{-1}(\tilde{\mathbf{z}})p=σ−1(z~)
  3. Scaling and Rounding: Scale by Δ\DeltaΔ and round to get integer coefficients

The inverse canonical embedding can be computed efficiently using the Vandermonde matrix:

V=(1ζ2nζ2n2⋯ζ2nn−11ζ2n3(ζ2n3)2⋯(ζ2n3)n−1⋮⋮⋮⋱⋮1ζ2n2n−1(ζ2n2n−1)2⋯(ζ2n2n−1)n−1)V = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \zeta_{2n} & \zeta_{2n}^2 & \cdots & \zeta_{2n}^{n-1} \\ 1 & \zeta_{2n}^3 & (\zeta_{2n}^3)^2 & \cdots & (\zeta_{2n}^3)^{n-1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & \zeta_{2n}^{2n-1} & (\zeta_{2n}^{2n-1})^2 & \cdots & (\zeta_{2n}^{2n-1})^{n-1} \end{pmatrix}V=​11⋮1​ζ2n​ζ2n3​⋮ζ2n2n−1​​ζ2n2​(ζ2n3​)2⋮(ζ2n2n−1​)2​⋯⋯⋱⋯​ζ2nn−1​(ζ2n3​)n−1⋮(ζ2n2n−1​)n−1​​

Then p=V−1z~\mathbf{p} = V^{-1} \tilde{\mathbf{z}}p=V−1z~, which can be computed using FFT in O(nlog⁡n)O(n \log n)O(nlogn) time.

Homomorphic Operations

Once we have polynomials encoding our complex vectors, homomorphic operations correspond to:

  • Addition: Enc(z1)+Enc(z2)=Enc(z1+z2)\text{Enc}(\mathbf{z}_1) + \text{Enc}(\mathbf{z}_2) = \text{Enc}(\mathbf{z}_1 + \mathbf{z}_2)Enc(z1​)+Enc(z2​)=Enc(z1​+z2​)
  • Multiplication: Enc(z1)⋅Enc(z2)=Enc(z1⊙z2)\text{Enc}(\mathbf{z}_1) \cdot \text{Enc}(\mathbf{z}_2) = \text{Enc}(\mathbf{z}_1 \odot \mathbf{z}_2)Enc(z1​)⋅Enc(z2​)=Enc(z1​⊙z2​)

where ⊙\odot⊙ is component-wise multiplication.

Discrete Gaussian Distributions and Noise

Here’s where we get into the “why is this secure?” part. The whole security of Ring-LWE comes down to adding the right kind of noise to our encryptions.

The Noise Intuition

Think of noise in encryption like static on a radio. If you’re trying to eavesdrop on a radio transmission, a little bit of static makes it hard to understand, but the intended recipient (who knows what to listen for) can still decode the message.

But here’s the crucial insight: not all noise is created equal. Random uniform noise would work, but it turns out that Gaussian noise is optimal for lattice-based cryptography. Why? Because it plays nicely with the geometric structure of lattices.

Discrete Gaussian Distribution: Nature’s Favorite Noise

The discrete Gaussian distribution DZ,σD_{\mathbb{Z}, \sigma}DZ,σ​ is like a bell curve, but restricted to integers. Picture a normal distribution centered at 0, and then only keep the probability mass at integer points.

The parameter σ\sigmaσ controls the “width” of the bell curve:

  • Small σ\sigmaσ: Noise is concentrated near 0 (good for correctness, bad for security)
  • Large σ\sigmaσ: Noise is spread out (good for security, bad for correctness)

Mathematically: Pr⁡[x]≈1σexp⁡(−πx2/σ2)\Pr[x] \approx \frac{1}{\sigma} \exp(-\pi x^2 / \sigma^2)Pr[x]≈σ1​exp(−πx2/σ2)

Why Gaussian? The Geometric Intuition

Here’s the deep reason why Gaussian noise is special: it’s the “most round” distribution.

When we add Gaussian noise to each coefficient of our polynomial, we’re essentially placing our secret in a “fuzzy cloud” that looks the same from every direction. This isotropy (rotational symmetry) is exactly what we need to make lattice problems hard.

If we used, say, uniform noise on {−B,…,B}\{-B, \ldots, B\}{−B,…,B}, our noise cloud would be cube-shaped, and cubes have corners where an attacker might find patterns. Gaussian clouds are perfectly round - no corners to exploit!

Error Polynomial Sampling

For Ring-LWE, we sample error polynomials e(X)=∑i=0n−1eiXie(X) = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} e_i X^ie(X)=∑i=0n−1​ei​Xi where each coefficient ei←DZ,σe_i \leftarrow D_{\mathbb{Z}, \sigma}ei​←DZ,σ​ independently.

The norm of the error polynomial in the canonical embedding is:

∥σ(e)∥∞=max⁡i∣e(ζ2n2i+1)∣\|\sigma(e)\|_{\infty} = \max_{i} |e(\zeta_{2n}^{2i+1})|∥σ(e)∥∞​=maxi​∣e(ζ2n2i+1​)∣

By concentration inequalities, with high probability:

∥σ(e)∥∞≤σnlog⁡n\|\sigma(e)\|_{\infty} \leq \sigma \sqrt{n \log n}∥σ(e)∥∞​≤σnlogn​

This bound is crucial for correctness - we need the noise to be small enough that decryption works, but large enough for security.

Security Foundations: Ideal Lattices

Now for the million-dollar question: why is Ring-LWE hard to break? The answer lies in geometry - specifically, the geometry of high-dimensional lattices.

The Lattice Intuition

Think of a lattice as a regular arrangement of points in space, like the integer grid Z2\mathbb{Z}^2Z2 in the plane. But instead of 2D, we’re working in nnn-dimensional space where nnn might be 1024 or 4096.

When we use Ring-LWE, we’re essentially hiding our secret in the lattice structure. The Ring-LWE samples give an adversary some information about which lattice we’re using, but not enough to reconstruct the secret.

From Polynomials to Geometric Points

Here’s the key connection: every polynomial p(X)=∑piXip(X) = \sum p_i X^ip(X)=∑pi​Xi corresponds to a point (p0,p1,…,pn−1)(p_0, p_1, \ldots, p_{n-1})(p0​,p1​,…,pn−1​) in nnn-dimensional space via the coefficient embedding.

The polynomial ring structure gives us a lattice with special properties - it’s not just any random lattice, but an ideal lattice with tons of symmetry. This extra structure is both a blessing (it makes things efficient) and potentially a curse (it might make attacks easier).

The Fundamental Lattice Problems

Breaking Ring-LWE is equivalent to solving one of these problems on ideal lattices:

  1. Shortest Vector Problem (SVP): Given a lattice, find the shortest non-zero vector
  2. Closest Vector Problem (CVP): Given a lattice and a target point, find the closest lattice point

In 2D, these problems are easy - you can just look at the lattice and see the answer. But in 1024-dimensional space? Good luck! The number of lattice points to check grows exponentially with dimension.

Worst-Case to Average-Case Reduction

Here’s the beautiful part: Lyubashevsky, Peikert, and Regev showed that solving Ring-LWE on average is as hard as solving worst-case problems on ideal lattices.

Specifically, there’s a quantum reduction from:

  • Worst-case: γ\gammaγ-approximate Shortest Vector Problem (SVP) on ideal lattices in Z[X]/(Xn+1)\mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^n + 1)Z[X]/(Xn+1)
  • Average-case: Ring-LWE with Gaussian error width σ=γ⋅poly(n)\sigma = \gamma \cdot \text{poly}(n)σ=γ⋅poly(n)

This gives us confidence that Ring-LWE is hard even against quantum adversaries (though the reduction is quantum).

Geometry of the Canonical Embedding

The canonical embedding σ\sigmaσ maps the ring to Cn\mathbb{C}^nCn, but we can view this as R2n\mathbb{R}^{2n}R2n by separating real and imaginary parts.

The key insight is that σ\sigmaσ is an isometry up to scaling: for any p∈Rp \in Rp∈R:

∥σ(p)∥22=n⋅∥p∥22\|\sigma(p)\|_2^2 = n \cdot \|p\|_2^2∥σ(p)∥22​=n⋅∥p∥22​

where ∥p∥22=∑i=0n−1pi2\|p\|_2^2 = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} p_i^2∥p∥22​=∑i=0n−1​pi2​ is the coefficient norm.

This geometric structure is what makes lattice algorithms like LLL and BKZ work on ideal lattices.

Parameter Selection and Trade-offs

Choosing parameters for Ring-LWE schemes involves balancing security, correctness, and efficiency.

Security Level

The security level depends on the root Hermite factor γ\gammaγ of the best known lattice attacks. For λ\lambdaλ-bit security, we need:

γ2n≥2λ/2\gamma^{2n} \geq 2^{\lambda / 2}γ2n≥2λ/2

Current estimates give γ≈1.0045\gamma \approx 1.0045γ≈1.0045 for BKZ attacks, so for 128-bit security:

n≥λ2log⁡2(γ)≈1282⋅0.0065≈10000n \geq \frac{\lambda}{2 \log_2(\gamma)} \approx \frac{128}{2 \cdot 0.0065} \approx 10000n≥2log2​(γ)λ​≈2⋅0.0065128​≈10000

But this is overly conservative. Practical parameter sets like those in Microsoft SEAL use n∈{1024,2048,4096,8192}n \in \{1024, 2048, 4096, 8192\}n∈{1024,2048,4096,8192} with carefully chosen moduli.

Modulus Selection

The ciphertext modulus qqq needs to be large enough to:

  1. Avoid Modular Reduction Errors: q>σn⋅2depthq > \sigma \sqrt{n} \cdot 2^{\text{depth}}q>σn​⋅2depth
  2. Enable NTT: q≡1(mod2n)q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}q≡1(mod2n) for efficient polynomial multiplication
  3. Resist Attacks: Not too small relative to nnn

A common approach is to use a product of primes: q=∏i=1kqiq = \prod_{i=1}^k q_iq=∏i=1k​qi​ where each qi≡1(mod2n)q_i \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}qi​≡1(mod2n).

Noise Growth Analysis

In CKKS, after ddd levels of multiplication, the noise magnitude grows roughly as:

Noise≈σ⋅nd/2⋅Bd\text{Noise} \approx \sigma \cdot n^{d/2} \cdot B^dNoise≈σ⋅nd/2⋅Bd

where BBB is the bound on message coefficients. This exponential growth limits the multiplicative depth.

Implementation Considerations

Number Theoretic Transform

Polynomial multiplication in Zq[X]/(Xn+1)\mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n + 1)Zq​[X]/(Xn+1) can be done efficiently using NTT. For this to work, we need:

  1. q≡1(mod2n)q \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}q≡1(mod2n)
  2. A primitive 2n2n2n-th root of unity ω\omegaω in Zq\mathbb{Z}_qZq​

Then NTT is just FFT over Zq\mathbb{Z}_qZq​, giving us O(nlog⁡n)O(n \log n)O(nlogn) polynomial multiplication.

Memory and Bandwidth

Ciphertext size is a practical concern. A Ring-LWE ciphertext consists of two polynomials, so 2nlog⁡q2n \log q2nlogq bits. For n=4096n = 4096n=4096 and log⁡q=200\log q = 200logq=200, that’s about 200KB per ciphertext.

Techniques like ciphertext packing and batching help amortize this cost across multiple plaintexts.

Precision and Scaling

CKKS uses fixed-point arithmetic, so precision loss accumulates. The precision budget determines how many operations you can perform before precision becomes unacceptable.

After each multiplication, you typically need to rescale by dividing by some factor (and rounding), which reduces the ciphertext modulus but maintains precision.

The math here gets pretty involved, but basically you’re managing a trade-off between precision, noise, and remaining multiplicative depth.

Looking Ahead

This foundation sets us up perfectly for understanding more advanced topics like:

  • Automorphisms: How to rotate encrypted data using Galois theory
  • Key Switching: Converting between different key representations
  • Bootstrapping: Refreshing noise to enable unlimited computation
  • Multi-Party Computation: Distributed computation on encrypted data

The algebraic structure we’ve explored - cyclotomic rings, canonical embeddings, discrete Gaussians - forms the mathematical backbone of all these techniques.

I have to say, working through this math really drives home how elegant modern cryptography is. Like, the fact that we can pack complex numbers into polynomials, perform arithmetic homomorphically, and base security on lattice problems - it’s just beautiful mathematics serving practical ends.

In my next post, I’ll dive into automorphisms and key switching, building directly on these foundations. The Galois group action on cyclotomic rings gives us this incredible ability to rotate encrypted data, but it comes with the price of changing the encryption key. Key switching is the technique that lets us convert back to the original key, and the math behind it is pretty wild.

Anyway, hope this gives you a solid mathematical foundation for understanding modern FHE schemes. There’s obviously way more depth here - ideal lattices, algebraic number theory, concrete security analysis - but this should be enough to follow the more advanced techniques.
