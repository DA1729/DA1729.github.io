Algebraic Structures You’ll Keep Seeing in FHE (One-Stop Reference)

#Abstract Algebra #Fully Homomorphic Encryption #Cryptography #Lattices

If you’ve started reading papers like TFHE or CKKS, you’ve probably seen words like ring, field, module, vector space, and torus thrown around casually, often without definitions.

When I was starting out, I’d get stuck re-Googling these over and over again while trying to follow a proof or a parameter setup.

In this blog, I’ve compiled clear, short definitions and examples of the most common algebraic structures used in modern FHE schemes. You can keep this open as a reference while reading papers — it’s not meant to be exhaustive, but practical for cryptography work.

1. Groups

A group consists of a set $( G )$ and a binary operation (usually $( + )$ or $( \cdot )$) such that:

  1. Closure: $( a \ast b \in G )$ for all $( a,b \in G )$.
  2. Associativity: $( (a \ast b) \ast c = a \ast (b \ast c) )$.
  3. Identity: There exists $( e \in G )$ such that $( a \ast e = e \ast a = a )$ for all $( a \in G )$.
  4. Inverses: For each $( a \in G )$, there exists $( a^{-1} )$ with $( a \ast a^{-1} = e )$.

If the operation is commutative ($( a \ast b = b \ast a )$), the group is abelian.

Examples in FHE:

  • $( (\mathbb{Z}, +) )$ is an abelian group.
  • The additive structure of a polynomial ring like $( \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n+1) )$ is a group under addition.

2. Rings

A ring $( R )$ has two operations:

  • Addition, under which $( R )$ is an abelian group.
  • Multiplication, which is associative and distributes over addition.

If multiplication is commutative, it’s a commutative ring. Some rings have a multiplicative identity (1), but not all.

Examples in FHE:

  • $( \mathbb{Z} )$ is a commutative ring.
  • $( \mathbb{Z}_q )$ is a ring used as plaintext or ciphertext space.
  • Polynomial rings $( \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n+1) )$ are the backbone of RLWE and RingLWE schemes.

3. Fields

A field is a commutative ring where:

  • Every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse.
  • Multiplication is commutative.

Basically: you can divide by anything except 0.

Examples in FHE:

  • $( \mathbb{Q} ), ( \mathbb{R} )$, and finite fields $( \mathbb{F}_p )$ are classic fields.
  • Ciphertext rings like $( \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n+1) )$ are not fields (not every element is invertible).

In CKKS, the plaintext space is embedded in a cyclotomic number field, and computations happen in its ring representation modulo $( q )$.

4. Vector Spaces

A vector space $( V )$ over a field $( F )$ is:

  • An abelian group under addition.
  • Equipped with scalar multiplication $( F \times V \to V )$ satisfying the usual distributive and associative rules.

Examples in FHE:

  • $( \mathbb{R}^n )$ over $( \mathbb{R} )$.
  • $( \mathbb{Q}^n )$ over $( \mathbb{Q} )$.

Lattice problems are often described in vector spaces over $( \mathbb{R} )$ (e.g., Gaussian distributions over $( \mathbb{R}^n )$).

5. Modules

A module generalizes a vector space by replacing the field with a ring.

A left module $( M )$ over a ring $( R )$ is:

  • An abelian group under addition.
  • Equipped with scalar multiplication $( R \times M \to M )$ obeying distributive and associative properties — but scalars come from a ring.

Why this matters:
Most ciphertext spaces in modern FHE (GLWE, RLWE, TLWE) are modules over polynomial rings.

Example: $( (T_N[X]^k, +, \cdot) ) is a ( \mathbb{Z}_N[X] )$-module.

6. The Torus

This one confuses almost everyone at first.

The real torus is defined as:
$$
[
\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R} \bmod 1 = \mathbb{R} / \mathbb{Z}.
]
$$
Think of it as the unit interval $([0,1))$ with addition modulo 1.

  • $( \mathbb{T} )$ is a group under addition.
  • It’s not a ring, because multiplication modulo 1 doesn’t behave well (e.g., $( 0 \times \tfrac{1}{2} )$ is not meaningful in $( \mathbb{T} )$).
  • But you can multiply integers by torus elements ⇒ making $( \mathbb{T} )$ a $( \mathbb{Z} )$-module.

Why torus matters in FHE:

  • TFHE and scale-invariant LWE encode plaintexts as torus elements (e.g., $( 0 )$ for bit 0, $( 1/2 )$ for bit 1).
  • Errors are small additive perturbations on the torus.

7. Putting It All Together

Recognizing what structure you’re in makes the notation in FHE papers much clearer.

Once you internalize these structures, reading FHE papers becomes much less intimidating. You’ll start noticing patterns:

  • TLWE lives on modules over the torus.
  • RLWE lives on polynomial rings.
  • CKKS operates in cyclotomic fields.
  • Gadget decompositions and bootstrapping are just module arithmetic with smart packing.

This blog is meant to be a living reference — I’ll keep adding structures and examples as I encounter them.
