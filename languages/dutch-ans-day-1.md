What better way to learn a language than reading the standard grammar book written in that language itself, lol. I present to you the ANS (and no, I am not typing out that big name more than once: *Algemene Nederlandse Spraakkunst*, there, done).

Okay, I just read a sentence:

```
De beschrijving van de klanken van een taal en van de systematiek waarmee ze
gebruikt worden is daarom een essentieel onderdeel van de grammatica van een taal.
```

Without any help, let me see if I can infer its meaning: "the explaining of *de* phonology of a language and of the systematic *waarmee* *ze* used word is *daarom* (no idea, 'indeed'?) an essential *onderdeel* (part) of the grammar of a language".

Obviously not fully correct (duh). The natural translation is: "The description of the sounds of a language and of the system according to which they are used is therefore an essential part of the grammar of a language."

I did not notice the *de* before *systematiek*. It is a really nice indicator that we have a noun, here the systematic organization.

Oh wow, *wordt* is not "word".

Okay, cool. Say I want to tell something about vertices and edges. Now I can say: *dit onderdeel van de wiskunde wordt grafentheorie genoemd*. ahahahhaa

And I think *worden*, *wordt*, *werden* (they change depending on the tense) are used in the passive, and *is* is used in the active. So let's try "graph theory is a branch of mathematics": *grafentheorie is een onderdeel van de wiskunde*.

Now I noticed something spooky: in the Dutch sentences I typed above, *worden* and *is* both show up back to back. Weird. Anyway, time shall teach us, hopefully.
